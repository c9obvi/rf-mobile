Important

The honest answer on “free” frequencies

With this UV-5R, there is no normal “free/no-license” U.S. voice channel you should transmit on. You can listen widely, and you can transmit legally on ham simplex/repeaters after an amateur radio license. FRS and MURS are license-free services, but they require FCC-certified FRS/MURS radios. GMRS is easy family comms, but it requires a GMRS license and GMRS-certified radios.

Best with these radios

Ham license

Use your UV-5Rs on 2m/70cm ham simplex and ham repeaters. Each transmitting operator needs a ham license.

Best no-license path

FRS radios

Buy actual FRS walkie-talkies. No license. 22 channels. Great for family/simple comms.

No-license VHF path

MURS radios

Buy certified MURS radios. No license. 5 VHF channels. Often quieter than FRS.

Family license

GMRS radios

Get a GMRS license and certified GMRS radios. No exam; good family range and repeaters.

Radio-to-radio setup

Use these recipes depending on what you actually want to do. The radio can technically enter many frequencies, but the legal transmit path matters more than what the keypad accepts.

Use after ham license

Simplex: both radios on the same ham frequency

  1. On both radios press VFO/MR until you are in frequency mode.
  2. Press A/B so you are editing the top line.
  3. Enter one clear simplex frequency. Start with 146.520 to call, then move to 146.550, 146.580, 147.510, or 446.500 if clear.
  4. Set tone OFF, offset OFF, LOW power first, and Narrow/Wide consistent on both radios.
  5. Push PTT, wait half a second, speak normally: “[callsign], radio check on 146.550.” Release to listen.
Free, but not on UV-5R

FRS is the clean no-license walkie-talkie route

Buy certified FRS radios and use channels 1–22. Do not transmit on FRS with a normal UV-5R. For practical family comms: pick FRS 1, 2, 3, or 4; avoid channel 16 if there is marine/harbor spillover confusion; use privacy codes only to reduce noise, not as privacy.

Free, but certified radios only

MURS is another no-license option

MURS gives you five VHF channels. This is often a solid “private property / small business / family” option if you buy certified MURS radios. Do not transmit on MURS with a normal UV-5R.

Life/property emergency only

Do not practice illegally “because emergency”

For normal practice, stay legal. In an immediate life/property emergency where normal communications are not available, keep transmissions brief, give location, situation, what help is needed, and who you are. Start with cell/911 first when possible.

MAYDAY / EMERGENCY
This is [name / callsign if licensed].
Location: [exact location].
Problem: [injury/fire/danger].
Needed: [medical/fire/rescue].
People: [number of people].
I will monitor this frequency.

“Most likely free to use” frequency sets

This section is split between free service and usable with your UV-5R. Those are not the same thing.

No license

FRS — best free family option

Use with certified FRS radios only. No license. 22 UHF channels. Channels 1–7 and 15–22 are up to 2 W on FRS; 8–14 are up to 0.5 W. Good for family, parks, road trips, events.

No license

MURS — free VHF option

Use with certified MURS radios only. No license. 5 VHF channels. Max 2 W. No repeaters. Often less crowded than FRS.

Not free, easy

GMRS — family license

Use with certified GMRS radios. License is for individuals/family; no exam. Usually the better “serious family comms” choice than FRS.

Best practical recommendation for you

Keep the Baofengs for listening + ham learning. For wife/family/event no-license talking, buy a pair/pack of FRS radios. If you want a better family setup later, get GMRS-certified radios plus a GMRS license. If you want to actually use these UV-5Rs for real radio-to-radio, get the ham Technician license and program the ham simplex set.

Chula Vista / Otay ham repeaters worth saving

Repeaters are not radio-to-radio direct; your radio transmits to a high site, then the repeater rebroadcasts. Use only with a ham license, identify with your callsign, and listen before transmitting.

Manual repeater recipe

  1. Enter the repeater output frequency, like 146.085.
  2. Set MENU 13 T-CTCS to the listed PL tone, like 100.0.
  3. Set MENU 25 SFT-D to + or -.
  4. Set MENU 26 OFFSET to 000.600 for 2m or 005.000 for 70cm.
  5. Save with MENU 27 MEM-CH. Clear an old memory first with MENU 28 DEL-CH.

Best first tests

For Chula Vista, try listening to 145.260, 146.085, and 146.640. With a ham license, key briefly and ask for a radio check. Don’t kerchunk repeatedly.

Useful listen-only stations

Weather

NOAA San Diego

162.400 KEC62 is your main weather station. Save it.

Marine weather

NOAA Marine

162.425 WNG637 covers San Diego Marine weather from Mount Soledad.

Do not TX

Marine Ch16

156.800 is distress/safety/calling. Listen only on a Baofeng.

Police/fire scanner reality

Chula Vista / San Diego public safety is not a good UV-5R target. Much of the region uses San Diego-Imperial County RCS NextGen Project 25 Phase II/trunked systems. A UV-5R cannot trunk-track P25 and cannot decrypt encrypted talkgroups. Use Broadcastify or a real digital trunking scanner for that world.

Manual UV-5R programming cheat sheet

Save a simplex channel

  1. VFO/MR to frequency mode.
  2. A/B to top line.
  3. Type frequency with no decimal: 146520.
  4. Set offset OFF, tones OFF, power LOW.
  5. Clear slot: MENU → 28 → MENU → channel → MENU → EXIT.
  6. Save slot: MENU → 27 → MENU → channel → MENU → EXIT.

Before importing

  1. Read from radio first in CHIRP and save a backup image.
  2. Import this CSV into the backup image, not directly to the radio.
  3. Confirm offsets/tones and disable TX on receive-only channels if your radio/software supports it.
